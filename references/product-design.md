# Product Design Interview — Grading Criteria

## What a Great Product Design Interview Looks Like

The candidate should demonstrate structured thinking, deep user empathy, and the ability to make and defend prioritization decisions. They should move fluently through a design framework without it feeling mechanical.

---

## Grading Dimensions (each scored 0–10)

### 1. Clarifying Questions (Weight: High)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate ask clarifying questions before jumping in?
- Did they clarify the product scope, user context, platform, and constraints?
- Did they avoid over-clarifying (asking too many unnecessary questions)?

**Strong**: Asks 2–4 targeted questions that meaningfully shape the answer. Confirms scope and constraints.
**Weak**: Jumps straight into the answer, or asks too many generic/irrelevant questions.

---

### 2. Goal & Context Setting (Weight: High)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate articulate the company's mission and how this product fits?
- Did they state what success looks like for this product?
- Did they tie the design to a business goal?

**Strong**: "For [Company], the north star is X. This product serves Y business objective, so we should optimize for Z."
**Weak**: Jumps into user segments or features without anchoring to company/product context.

---

### 3. User Segmentation (Weight: High)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate identify distinct user segments?
- Did they prioritize one segment and explain why?
- Was the rationale tied to company goals, not just personal preference?

**Strong**: Lists 2–3 segments, explains tradeoffs, picks one with a clear business rationale.
**Weak**: Only mentions one user type, or picks a segment without explaining why.

---

### 4. User Pain Points / Needs (Weight: High)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate articulate specific, concrete pain points for the chosen user?
- Were these grounded in empathy and real user behavior (not assumed features)?
- Did they prioritize pain points?

**Strong**: Lists 3–5 specific pain points with reasoning. Prioritizes 1–2 most important ones.
**Weak**: Lists vague needs ("they want something easy to use") or jumps straight to solutions.

---

### 5. Solutions / Feature Ideas (Weight: Medium)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate generate multiple distinct solution ideas?
- Were solutions diverse (not just incremental variations of the same idea)?
- Were they tied to the pain points identified earlier?

**Strong**: Proposes 3+ creative, distinct solutions. Each maps to a specific pain point.
**Weak**: Proposes 1–2 solutions, or solutions that don't connect to the user needs stated earlier.

---

### 6. Prioritization (Weight: High)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate prioritize their solutions using a clear framework?
- Did they consider impact vs. effort, feasibility, and alignment with company goals?
- Did they clearly state which solution to build first and why?

**Strong**: Uses an explicit framework (impact/effort, RICE, etc.), makes a clear recommendation with rationale.
**Weak**: Chooses a solution without explaining why, or tries to build everything.

---

### 7. Success Metrics (Weight: Medium)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate define how they'd measure success?
- Did they name specific metrics tied to user outcomes and business goals?
- Did they mention guardrail metrics?

**Strong**: Names 2–3 primary metrics (e.g., DAU, task completion rate), 1–2 guardrail metrics, explains why each matters.
**Weak**: Only mentions a vanity metric (e.g., "downloads"), or skips metrics entirely.

---

### 8. Structure & Communication (Weight: Medium)
**What to look for:**
- Was the answer well-organized and easy to follow?
- Did the candidate use signposting ("First I'll... then I'll...")?
- Did they check in with the interviewer appropriately?

**Strong**: Clear signposting, logical flow, appropriate pacing, checks in before going deep.
**Weak**: Jumps between topics, hard to follow, no signposting.

---

### 9. Depth vs. Breadth Balance (Weight: Low)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate go deep enough on the most important sections?
- Did they avoid spending too long on less important parts?

**Strong**: Spends ~60% of time on pain points, solutions, and prioritization. Moves quickly through context-setting.
**Weak**: Spends too long on clarifying questions or user segmentation and rushes through solutions.

---

### 10. Handling Pushback / Follow-ups (Weight: Medium — only if applicable)
**What to look for:**
- If the interviewer pushed back, did the candidate defend their choices confidently?
- Did they update their view when presented with new information?
- Did they avoid caving immediately under pressure?

**Strong**: Acknowledges the pushback, engages with it thoughtfully, either defends or genuinely updates.
**Weak**: Immediately agrees with the interviewer, or digs in defensively without engaging with the point.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Feature factory**: Jumping to solutions without understanding users first
- **No prioritization**: Treating all solutions as equally worth building
- **Generic users**: Describing users too broadly ("millennials", "busy professionals")
- **Metric-less success**: Not defining how you'd know the product worked
- **No company context**: Designing in a vacuum without anchoring to the company's goals
- **Over-clarifying**: Spending too long asking questions before starting

---

## Score Benchmarks

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | Hire signal: structured, user-centric, makes sharp prioritization decisions, no major gaps |
| 7–8 | Strong candidate with minor gaps (e.g., metrics are weak, or one section was rushed) |
| 5–6 | Borderline: some good instincts but missing key components or depth |
| 3–4 | Significant gaps: weak structure, jumps to solutions, doesn't prioritize |
| 0–2 | Major issues: no framework, no user empathy, confused throughout |
