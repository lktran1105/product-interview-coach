# Product Strategy Interview — Grading Criteria

## What a Great Product Strategy Interview Looks Like

The candidate should think like a business owner. They should demonstrate the ability to assess a market, understand a company's position, make long-horizon bets, and connect those bets to near-term execution. They should balance ambition with realism.

---

## Grading Dimensions (each scored 0–10)

### 1. Problem / Opportunity Framing (Weight: High)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate clearly articulate the strategic question or opportunity before diving in?
- Did they scope the problem appropriately — not too narrow, not too broad?
- Did they restate or reframe the prompt to show strategic thinking?

**Strong**: Reframes the question in terms of the company's core challenge. "This is really a question about whether [Company] should double down on X or expand into Y — let me think through that."
**Weak**: Accepts the prompt at face value and starts listing ideas without framing the strategic tension.

---

### 2. Company & Competitive Context (Weight: High)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate demonstrate knowledge of the company's mission, business model, and current position?
- Did they reference the competitive landscape?
- Did they identify the company's key strengths and vulnerabilities?

**Strong**: References the company's business model, key revenue streams, and 1–2 major competitors. Identifies what the company is uniquely positioned to do.
**Weak**: Generic statements ("they're a big tech company") or no competitive analysis at all.

---

### 3. Market & User Insight (Weight: High)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate identify relevant market trends, user behaviors, or tailwinds/headwinds?
- Did they size the opportunity meaningfully (even roughly)?
- Did they connect market dynamics to the strategic recommendation?

**Strong**: Names 2–3 market trends relevant to the question. Makes a back-of-envelope size estimate. Explains what this means strategically.
**Weak**: Ignores market context, or describes market trends without connecting them to the recommendation.

---

### 4. Strategic Options Generation (Weight: Medium)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate generate multiple strategic paths/options?
- Were the options genuinely distinct (not just incremental variations)?
- Did they cover the meaningful solution space?

**Strong**: Proposes 3 clearly distinct strategic options (e.g., "build, buy, or partner"; "go deep in core vs. expand horizontally vs. enter new vertical").
**Weak**: Only considers one path, or options are so similar they're not real alternatives.

---

### 5. Recommendation & Rationale (Weight: High)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate make a clear, specific recommendation?
- Was the recommendation tied to the company's strengths and the market opportunity?
- Did they explain what they're saying *no* to and why?

**Strong**: "I recommend Option B because [company strength] + [market tailwind] = [sustainable advantage]. This means deprioritizing Option A, which would dilute focus."
**Weak**: Hedges ("it depends"), recommends everything, or makes a recommendation without a clear rationale.

---

### 6. Risk & Tradeoff Analysis (Weight: Medium)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate acknowledge the risks of their recommendation?
- Did they name what they're giving up by not choosing the alternatives?
- Did they have a mitigation plan for the biggest risk?

**Strong**: Identifies 2–3 real risks, names the biggest tradeoff, suggests how to mitigate or monitor.
**Weak**: No risk discussion, or only surface-level ("execution is always hard").

---

### 7. Execution Path / Milestones (Weight: Medium)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate outline what "executing on this strategy" would look like?
- Did they identify near-term milestones or decision points?
- Did they mention what needs to be true for this strategy to succeed?

**Strong**: Describes 3–6 month horizon actions, names 1–2 key assumptions to validate early, mentions what the first signal of success/failure would be.
**Weak**: Strategy stays at the abstract level with no path to execution.

---

### 8. Success Metrics (Weight: Medium)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate define how they'd measure whether the strategy is working?
- Are the metrics strategic (market share, revenue per user, retention) rather than just product metrics?
- Did they note leading vs. lagging indicators?

**Strong**: Names 2–3 strategic metrics, distinguishes between leading signals (early indicator) and lagging outcomes (long-term proof).
**Weak**: Only mentions product-level metrics (e.g., DAU) for a strategic question, or skips metrics entirely.

---

### 9. Communication & Structure (Weight: Medium)
**What to look for:**
- Was the argument easy to follow?
- Did the candidate use a clear structure (context → options → recommendation → risks)?
- Did they adjust their depth based on interviewer cues?

**Strong**: Answer has a clear through-line. Interviewee signals where they're going before they get there.
**Weak**: Jumps between ideas, argument is hard to follow, no clear structure.

---

### 10. Intellectual Rigor & Pushback Handling (Weight: Medium)
**What to look for:**
- Did the candidate engage with complexity rather than oversimplify?
- When pushed, did they defend their reasoning or thoughtfully update?
- Did they acknowledge what they don't know?

**Strong**: Engages with hard parts of the question. Comfortable saying "I'm uncertain about X, so I'd want to validate that assumption early."
**Weak**: Oversimplifies, caves under pushback, or doubles down without engaging with the new information.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Listing without recommending**: Naming 3 options but refusing to pick one
- **Missing company context**: Strategy that could apply to any company
- **Feature-level thinking**: Answering a strategy question with product features
- **No tradeoffs**: Presenting only upside with no acknowledgment of risk or cost
- **No execution bridge**: A great vision with no path to getting there
- **Vague metrics**: "We'd track engagement" without defining what that means

---

## Score Benchmarks

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | Strong hire: sharp framing, bold recommendation with clear rationale, handles complexity |
| 7–8 | Good candidate: solid structure, clear rec, minor gaps in rigor or market insight |
| 5–6 | Borderline: some strategic thinking but hedges too much or misses key dimensions |
| 3–4 | Weak: feature-level thinking, no clear recommendation, or ignores company context |
| 0–2 | Not ready: no strategic framework, confused by the question, no coherent argument |
